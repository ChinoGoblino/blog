On Keeping Notes

There’s a particular kind of note that almost writes itself. You’re in the middle of something else — reading, talking, staring at a problem — and a thought arrives fully formed. You write it down. Later you return and it still makes sense. Those notes are rare.

Most notes are messier than that. They’re half-formed, referential, full of personal shorthand that decays in meaning within weeks. The question isn’t how to take perfect notes. It’s how to build a practice where even imperfect notes are useful.

Why most note systems fail

The obvious answer is that people stop using them. But that explanation defers the question: why do people stop?

I think the friction is almost always at the retrieval end, not the capture end. Capturing a note is easy and feels productive. Retrieving one — finding the exact thing you half-remember writing six months ago — is harder, and the failure is invisible until the moment you need it.

If your retrieval experience is bad, your notes eventually stop feeling useful, and you stop adding to the system, even if the capture experience was pleasant.

What actually works

Plain text files

I have notes going back fifteen years in plain text files. I can open any of them in any editor, search them with grep, and I’m not dependent on any application staying alive or any sync service staying solvent.

The downside is obvious: no automatic linking, no backlinks, no graph view. I’ve found those features less valuable than promised. What matters is that the files are still there.

A single inbox

One place where new things land. Not organized by topic — just chronological. Sorting happens later, or not at all. The inbox is where you capture without friction. If you have to decide where something goes before you write it, you’ll sometimes not write it.

Search over structure

Folders and tags are fine, but search is more forgiving. If a note is findable, it can live almost anywhere. Build your practice around retrieval, not organization.

The actual constraint

The best note-taking system is the one you keep using. Optimization should follow sustained use, not precede it. Start with what’s immediately useful; everything else is theory.
